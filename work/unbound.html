UNBOUND

Designing a contemporary redesign of the QAGOMA website for art discovery and cultural engagement.

UNBOUND is an academic web design project where I redesigned the QAGOMA website as a more expressive and accessible digital experience for discovering exhibitions, events, gallery content, and cultural stories.

Overview

UNBOUND is a conceptual redesign of the QAGOMA (Queensland Art Gallery | Gallery of Modern Art) website, developed as an academic project for DECO7140 Introduction to Web Design. The project reimagines how a major cultural institution's digital presence could feel more expressive, accessible, and engaging for contemporary audiences — transforming the website from a static information source into an immersive digital experience that reflects the energy and visual richness of contemporary art.

Design Challenge

⚡ The central question is how to design a cultural platform that balances visual immersion with functional clarity — making contemporary art feel more discoverable without sacrificing the practical goals of browsing exhibitions, finding events, reading artist stories, and booking tickets.

Tools & Skills

Used Figma for interface planning and layout design, Adobe Photoshop and Illustrator for visual assets, image treatment, branding elements, and graphic direction, and HTML, CSS, and JavaScript for the final front-end prototype. This work drew on skills in UX/UI design, editorial visual identity development, responsive web development, information architecture, accessibility implementation (semantic HTML, ARIA labels, alt text), and modular CSS architecture — balancing design expression with technical execution.

Research Scope

Analysed how gallery, museum, and cultural event websites organise visual content and guide users toward action. Studied the presentation of exhibitions, events, artist profiles, newsletters, and editorial content across multiple cultural institutions, paying close attention to where users lose context or struggle to move between sections. This informed an information architecture built around five core areas: Home, Gallery, Events, Blog, and About Us.

Methodology

The project moved through research and analysis, information architecture, early prototyping, visual language development, and front-end implementation across 12 weeks. Early prototyping mapped key user journeys: discovering a featured exhibition, browsing gallery content, exploring upcoming events, reading cultural stories, and moving toward ticket booking. The visual direction was built around a bold black-and-white interface that lets artwork and performance imagery provide the colour and emotion — using large typography, strong contrast, editorial spacing, and oversized imagery. Navigation design prioritised clarity through repeated interaction patterns: underlined links, large call-to-action buttons, carousel browsing, and hover states. The final responsive prototype was developed with modular stylesheets and accessibility considerations throughout.

Industry Relevance

How do you create a digital experience that honours the visual drama of art while still being functionally accessible and easy to navigate?

As cultural institutions invest in digital transformation, design teams face a persistent tension. This project demonstrates strategies such as using a monochrome interface to let the art itself drive the visual experience, designing navigation that is both minimal and predictable, and treating the homepage as a digital exhibition poster that invites exploration rather than overwhelming with options.
