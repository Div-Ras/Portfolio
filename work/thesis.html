Master’s Thesis UX Research Media Architecture

From fragments to flow

A user-centred media architecture thesis exploring how fragmented urban spaces can feel more connected, memorable, and human.

This project became my way of bringing together architecture, UX research, and interaction design. Set around Kurilpa Bridge in Brisbane, it looks at how small moments of light, sound, and participation can shift a place from being just a route into something people notice, remember, and quietly share.

Overview

This thesis examines urban fragmentation through the lens of media architecture and user-centred design, focusing on Kurilpa Bridge in Brisbane — a space that is physically well-connected but experientially disconnected. Using a Research through Design approach, the project moved from site observation and contextual inquiry into concept development, prototyping, and evaluation, culminating in Residues of Passage: a light-and-sound prototype that makes passing human presence more visible over time.

Research Challenge

⚡ The central question is how media architecture can create a sense of shared presence in transient public spaces — without forcing interaction, creating spectacle, or disrupting the existing rhythm of the site.

Tools & Skills

Applied contextual inquiry, behavioural mapping, proxemic analysis, semi-structured interviews, and mixed-method evaluation (survey, design discussion, observation). Tools included Qualtrics, Google Colab for data analysis, HTML/CSS/JavaScript for the digital component, and physical prototyping with light-and-sound interaction. This work drew on skills in spatial research, interaction design, Research through Design methodology, sensory prototyping, and user-centred evaluation — balancing qualitative depth with site-responsive design thinking.

Research Scope

Conducted a year-long investigation grounded in media architecture, urban interaction, and human-centred design theory. Research included extensive site observation sessions at Kurilpa Bridge, behavioural mapping across multiple timeframes, proxemic studies of pedestrian movement, and interviews with bridge users — all feeding into an iterative design cycle that moved from insight to prototype to evaluation.

Methodology

The research followed a two-stage Research through Design process. Stage 1 focused on understanding the site through contextual inquiry, observation, and interviews, identifying that people notice sensory qualities (light, wind, views, other passers-by) but only briefly. Stage 2 translated these insights into Residues of Passage — a glowing orb prototype that plays anonymous audio traces left by previous visitors. A mixed-method evaluation with participants explored how people approached the object, what made the interaction meaningful, and where the concept needed refinement. Treating urban space as a design material reframed the bridge from a transit route into a site of quiet, layered participation.

Industry Relevance

As cities invest in smart infrastructure and public interaction, designers face a critical question: How can technology enhance the experience of public space without overriding its character? The research demonstrates strategies such as designing for ambient presence rather than active engagement, using sensory feedback that respects the pace of a place, and positioning technology as a medium for subtle human connection rather than a destination in itself.
