Project Overview

This Advanced HCI project looks at why cultural and natural heritage can remain vulnerable even when protection frameworks already exist. I focused on how heritage sites, indigenous knowledge, climate stress, governance, tourism pressure, and illicit trade influence one another.

The outcome is a research website supported by a Miro systems map. The website gives context, while the map makes the relationships between causes, risks, stakeholders, and interventions easier to explore.

Challenge

The main challenge was that the topic could not be explained as one simple problem. Heritage loss is shaped by funding, policy, climate, tourism, cultural ownership, community involvement, and illegal markets.

I needed to make the research easier to follow without removing the complexity. The systems map helped me keep the bigger picture visible.

Research & Insights

The research showed that heritage protection depends on connected systems. Policy matters, but it is not enough if local communities are excluded, funding is weak, or climate risks keep increasing.

A key insight was that cultural heritage is not only a physical site or object. It is also tied to memory, identity, knowledge, place, and community. Systems thinking helped show those wider relationships.

Systems Map

The Miro board presents the project as a system rather than a flat research report. It connects stakeholders, causes, risks, feedback loops, and leverage points so the issue can be explored from different angles.

This helped me understand where change could happen, such as better community participation, stronger documentation, improved policy enforcement, and more careful use of digital technologies.

Research Website

The website was created to organise the research into clear sections: overview, research, systems map, and AI acknowledgement. This made the project easier to navigate and gave the systems map more context.

The design was kept simple so the focus stayed on the research. The website acts as a supporting layer for the Miro board, explaining what the issue is, why it matters, and how the system is connected.

Outcome

This project helped me use HCI as a way to make complex research more understandable. Instead of only writing about cultural heritage vulnerability, I translated the research into a visual and interactive structure.

The final work shows how systems thinking can support design research by making hidden relationships easier to see, question, and discuss.
