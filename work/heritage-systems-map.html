Overview

This Advanced HCI project examines why cultural and natural heritage can remain vulnerable even when protection frameworks already exist. Using systems thinking as a design research method, it maps the complex relationships between heritage sites, indigenous knowledge, climate pressure, governance, tourism, community involvement, and illicit trade — translating dense research into an interactive, explorable structure.

Research Challenge

⚡ The central question is how HCI and systems thinking can make the complexity of cultural heritage vulnerability more visible and understandable — without oversimplifying the interconnected causes, risks, and stakeholders involved.

Tools & Skills

Used Miro for systems mapping and visual synthesis, alongside HTML, CSS, and JavaScript for the supporting research website. This work drew on skills in HCI research, systems thinking methodology, research synthesis, web design, causal loop mapping, and stakeholder analysis — translating dense academic research into a navigable visual and interactive format.

Research Scope

Conducted a research synthesis across cultural heritage protection, indigenous knowledge systems, climate change impacts, governance frameworks, tourism pressure, community participation, and illicit trade networks. The research revealed that heritage protection depends on deeply connected systems — policy alone is insufficient if local communities are excluded, funding is weak, or climate risks continue to escalate. A key insight was that cultural heritage is not only a physical site or object; it is tied to memory, identity, knowledge, place, and community.

Methodology

The project followed a systems-thinking approach within an Advanced HCI framework. Research was synthesised into a Miro systems map that connects stakeholders, causes, risks, feedback loops, and leverage points, allowing the issue to be explored from multiple angles rather than read as a flat report. A companion research website was built to organise findings into clear sections — overview, research, systems map, and AI acknowledgement — giving the interactive map contextual depth. The design was kept deliberately simple so the focus remained on the research itself, with the website acting as a supporting layer that explains what the issue is, why it matters, and how the system connects.

Industry Relevance

As heritage institutions, policy-makers, and communities face accelerating environmental and social pressures, research communication faces a critical gap: How do you make the complexity of heritage vulnerability accessible to diverse stakeholders without flattening the problem? This project demonstrates strategies such as using systems mapping to reveal hidden relationships and feedback loops, designing research outputs as interactive explorations rather than static documents, and positioning HCI as a bridge between academic research and broader public understanding.
